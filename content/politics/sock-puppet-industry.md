## Imagine You Run a Small Charity

**You have a noble cause - clearer food labeling, or maybe getting sugary drinks out of schools. Your staff are passionate. Your research is thorough. Your total private donations for the year: close to zero. But a government department has a grant program that aligns with your mission. The application is 200 pages. The criteria specify the conclusion the research should reach. You spend three months on the application. You get the grant. Of the original amount, about a third reaches your actual work. The rest went to the committees that processed your application, the consultants who helped you write it, and the monitoring visits they require. You know this. You also know that if you refuse the money, your charity closes.**

This is not a story about one bad grant. It is a story about how the entire system works, in every Western country, for thousands of grants, year after year.

The government gives money to a quango. The quango distributes it in smaller packets to specialist committees. The committees issue calls for proposals that specify what conclusion the research should reach. Charities spend months writing applications. The paperwork consumes most of the grant before the work begins. The output is a report that tells the government what it already wanted to hear. Everyone involved is a good person doing what they believe is right. The system itself is the problem.

And the system is not an accident.

## The Pipeline

The money follows a predictable path. It is the same in London, Ottawa, Canberra, and Brussels.

**Layer one: the department.** A government ministry has a policy goal - reduce obesity, fight climate change, promote diversity. It knows it needs "independent" voices to make the case. It cannot rely solely on its own officials, because that would look like propaganda. So it funds the appearance of a grassroots movement.

**Layer two: the quango.** The department cannot give money directly without looking like it is buying influence. So it passes the funds to a non-departmental public body - a quango - that exists to distribute grants. The quango adds its own overhead, its own application forms, its own monitoring requirements, and its own strategic priorities.

**Layer three: the specialist committee.** The quango convenes a panel of experts to evaluate grant applications. The panel includes academics who have built their careers on the same assumptions the grant is designed to confirm. Conflict of interest is not a concern here - it's absence is a feature. The panel wants to fund research that supports its worldview, because that worldview is the source of its members' professional standing.

**Layer four: the charity.** The charity writes the application, attends the interviews, submits the monitoring reports, and produces the final research. The conclusion was never in doubt. The grant specified it. The committee expected it. The department needs it. The charity cannot afford to surprise anyone, because next year's grant depends on this year's compliance.

Each layer takes its cut. Not in bribes - in salaries, office space, meeting expenses, consultants, application software, monitoring frameworks, evaluation reports, and the endless documentation that proves the money was spent responsibly.

By the time the research reaches the minister's desk, most of the original amount has been consumed by the process of distributing it. The output is a report that could have been written before the grant was awarded.

## The Sock Puppet Report

The most honest analysis of this system ever published is a 2012 report from the Institute of Economic Affairs titled, bluntly, **"Sock Puppets: How the Government Lobbies Itself and Why."**

The report's central finding: when government funds charities to lobby the same government, it is subverting democracy. The charities become extensions of the state, advocating for policies the state has already decided to pursue, while maintaining the appearance of independent civil society voices.

The author, Christopher Snowdon, documented the scale of the problem. He found that government-funded charities and pressure groups consistently lobby for:
- Bigger government
- Higher taxes
- More regulation
- New enforcement agencies

The pattern is not subtle. The organizations that receive government money reliably call for more government power. This is not because they are corrupt. It is because the grant system selects for organizations that already agree with the government's direction. An organization that opposed the government's policy priorities would not receive the grant in the first place.

In 2012, this was a niche report. In 2026, the data is worse.

## The 99% Club

In May 2026, the Taxpayers' Alliance published an assessment of taxpayer funding for major UK charities. The headline number: **21 charities received at least 99 percent of their income from government sources.**

Read that again. Twenty-one organizations that the public thinks of as independent charities are almost entirely funded by the state. They have virtually no private donations. They exist because the government pays them to exist. And what do they advocate for? More government spending, more regulation, and more power for the departments that fund them.

The total figure is staggering. Large UK charities reported receiving £12.4 billion from government sources in 2024 - £7.9 billion in contracts and £4.5 billion in grants. That is more than the GDP of dozens of countries, flowing from the state to organizations that exist to lobby the state.

The Taxpayers' Alliance report showed the NCVO's own data: 26 percent of all UK charity income comes from government. For the charities that do the most political advocacy, the figure is far higher.

Ask yourself: if a charity gets 99 percent of its money from the government, who is it really working for?

## The European Commission Pays Its Own Lobbyists

The same pattern exists at the EU level, and it tries even harder to disguise it.

In 2025, Politico Europe reported that the European Commission had been paying environmental NGOs to lobby for the European Green Deal - including lobbying other departments of the European Commission itself. MEPs from the European People's Party documented that the Commission's funding programs were being used to create a network of advocacy groups that would pressure EU institutions to adopt the Commission's preferred policies.

The scale is massive. The EU's LIFE program alone distributes hundreds of millions of euros to environmental NGOs. The requirement to be eligible for funding: the organization must support the EU's climate goals. An NGO that questioned whether the Green Deal was the right approach would not be funded. An NGO that called for even more ambitious targets and faster implementation would be first in line.

The European Court of Auditors called the funding system "opaque." In July 2025, the Commission announced it would deny funding for health policy advocacy due to "reputational risk" - a quiet admission that the whole arrangement looked like what it was.

## The Canadian Pattern

Canada offers a clean before-and-after case study.

In 2009, the Harper government defunded KAIROS, a faith-based ecumenical organization that received 39 percent of its $4 million budget from the Canadian International Development Agency (CIDA). The government's justification was that KAIROS's advocacy work on Palestinian issues violated the terms of its grant. KAIROS's defenders said the government was punishing an organization that disagreed with its foreign policy.

Both sides were right. That is the point.

The Harper government was accused of politicizing the grant process - cutting funds to groups that opposed its policies. But the question nobody asked is whether the previous government had politicized the process by funding those groups in the first place. If a government cannot defund an organization when its advocacy conflicts with government policy, the organization is not independent. It is a permanent opposition funded by the state.

When Trudeau came to power, KAIROS was quietly re-funded. The organization had not changed its mission. The government had changed its politics. The grant process was revealed as what it had always been: a mechanism for funding political allies.

## The United States: Research with a Conclusion

In the United States, the mechanism operates through research grants rather than direct charity funding, but the effect is the same.

For decades, the Dickey Amendment prohibited the Centers for Disease Control from funding research that advocated for gun control. In 2018, the amendment was reinterpreted to allow the CDC to fund gun violence research - but only research that treated gun violence as a public health crisis requiring government intervention.

The result was predictable. The CDC awarded millions of dollars in grants to researchers and organizations that already believed gun control was the answer. Research proposals that questioned whether more laws would reduce violence were not funded. Research proposals that assumed the answer and set out to prove it were funded generously. The same pattern appears in the National Institutes of Health's funding of research on obesity, sugar taxes, and every other politically relevant topic.

The American version lacks the quango layer, but the pipeline is the same: government money flows to researchers who will produce the conclusions the government needs to justify the policies it wants.

## The Charity That Cannot Say No

This brings us back to the charity in the opening paragraph.

My friend runs it. She is good at her job. She got an MBE for her work on food labeling. She genuinely believes the government should be doing more to regulate what goes into packaged food. She does not see her grant as buying her advocacy. She sees it as funding the research that supports her existing beliefs.

She is not wrong about her beliefs. She may be right about food labeling. That is not the point.

The point is that her charity has virtually no private donations. They do not fundraise from the public. They do not have a significant donor base. They exist because the government pays them to exist, and they will exist only as long as the government agrees with what they do. If the government changed its position on food labeling tomorrow, the grant would disappear and the charity would close. Not because the cause was wrong. Because the cause was dependent.

This is the deepest corruption of the grant system. It does not buy explicit loyalty. It creates dependency so complete that dissent becomes impossible. The charity cannot advocate against the government's priorities, because those priorities are the source of its funding. It cannot refuse a grant with strings attached, because without the grant it ceases to exist.

"We can always refuse the money" is a statement that only someone who has never depended on the money would make.

## The Real Cost

The waste is the point. Or at least, it is a feature, not a bug.

Every hour spent writing a 200-page grant application is an hour not spent on the charity's actual mission. Every committee meeting to evaluate applications is a day of salaries that could have funded actual work. Every monitoring report is a week of labor that goes to documentation, not results. Every consultant hired to improve the next application's chances is a slice of the grant that will never reach a beneficiary.

Most of the money is consumed by the process of distributing the money. The final report is almost incidental. It is the cover story that justifies the pipeline.

The government does not fix this because it does not want to. The pipeline serves three purposes:

1. **It creates the appearance of independent support for government policy.** A minister can stand up and say "the charity sector agrees" without mentioning that the charity sector is on the payroll.

2. **It absorbs political pressure.** Charities that are busy writing grant applications cannot organize protests. Organizations that depend on government funding do not bite the hand that feeds them.

3. **It distributes patronage without looking like patronage.** Jobs, salaries, and career paths are created for people who share the government's worldview, all under the banner of civil society.

The real cost is not only the billions in grants. The real cost is that there is no independent civil society. There are organizations that look independent, sound independent, and are treated by the media as independent - but depend on the state for their existence.

## What Would Actual Independence Look Like?

The test is simple. Does the charity have a donor base that would sustain it without government money?

If the answer is no, it is not a charity. It is an extension of the state. It may do good work. Its staff may be dedicated. Its research may be honest. But it is not independent, and it will not advocate against the source of its funding.

The Sock Puppets report proposed banning government funding of political advocacy by charities. That is one solution. A simpler one: any charity that receives more than half its income from government should be required to say so in every public statement. Not in a footnote on their website. In every press release, every report, every media appearance.

"Funded primarily by the Department of X."

Let them say it out loud.

## The Question

The next time you hear a politician cite a "charity report" calling for more regulation, ask yourself: did that charity choose to research this topic, or did the government pay them to?

The next time you hear an "independent study" demanding higher taxes or new government programs, ask: who funded it, and could the researchers have reached any other conclusion and kept their funding?

The next time someone tells you their charity is fighting for a better world, ask: who signs their paycheque?

The answer is the same in London, Ottawa, Canberra, and Brussels. It is the government. It has always been the government. And the machine will keep running as long as the money keeps flowing - from the department, through the quango, past the committee, and into the bank account of the organization that can never say no.

**Are you going to keep pretending the "independent voices" are independent?**
