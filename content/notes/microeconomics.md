## What It Is

Microeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the behavior of individual economic agents - households, firms, and consumers - and how they interact in specific markets.

The prefix *micro* means small. Microeconomics looks at the trees, not the forest. It asks:

- Why does a consumer choose one product over another?
- How does a firm decide how much to produce?
- What determines the price of a specific good?
- How do wages, rents, and profits get set in a particular industry?

These questions are answered using a small set of core tools: supply and demand, marginal analysis, opportunity cost, and the concept of equilibrium.

## The Core Questions

**How do people choose?** Microeconomics models individuals as making decisions at the margin: *If I buy one more unit, what do I gain, and what do I give up?* This is marginal analysis. It applies to everything - buying a coffee, hiring a worker, building a factory.

**How do prices form?** In a market, prices emerge from the interaction of buyers (demand) and sellers (supply). The price adjusts until the quantity buyers want equals the quantity sellers are willing to supply. This is market equilibrium.

**How do different market structures behave?** A farmer selling wheat faces different constraints than a software company or a pharmaceutical patent holder. Microeconomics studies how markets behave under different conditions - perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition - and how the outcomes differ.

**How does the economy allocate resources?** Through prices, profits, and losses. Microeconomics shows how these signals direct resources toward their most valued uses - or, when they are distorted (price controls, subsidies, externalities), away from them.

## Why It Matters Separately from Macroeconomics

Microeconomics and macroeconomics are not competing theories. They look at different scales.

Microeconomics provides the foundation. Every macroeconomic phenomenon - inflation, unemployment, economic growth - is the aggregate of millions of microeconomic decisions. You cannot understand what happens to "the price level" without understanding how individual prices are set. You cannot understand unemployment without understanding how individual firms decide to hire.

But the whole is not simply the sum of its parts. Aggregate phenomena can behave differently from individual ones - a paradox known as the **fallacy of composition** (what is true for one can be false for all). One farmer who grows more wheat benefits. All farmers who grow more wheat drive the price down and may all be worse off.

## Common Misunderstandings

**Microeconomics does not assume people are perfectly rational.** Modern microeconomics incorporates behavioral insights - people are biased, impulsive, and susceptible to framing. The models are tools for understanding tendencies, not descriptions of actual psychology.

**"Marginal" does not mean "small."** In microeconomics, marginal means *additional* - the next unit, the next hour, the next dollar. It is a way of thinking about choices, not a comment on their importance.
